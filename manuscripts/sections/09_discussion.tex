
\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{The Teacher-Choice Principle}

The ablation study reveals a clear rank ordering: Calibrated UAS teacher ($9.82$) $\succ$ UAS teacher ($11.14$) $\succ$ scratch ($5{,}451$).
The $1.32$-unit gap between calibrated and uncalibrated teachers exceeds the effect of every architectural modification (log-normalization, zero-init), establishing teacher quality as the dominant design choice among the tested training variants.

This finding has a natural interpretation.
Behavior cloning initializes the policy in a basin of attraction determined by the teacher's action distribution.
\reinforce{} fine-tuning then makes local improvements within this basin.
A stronger teacher produces a better starting basin, and the resulting improvements from fine-tuning compound rather than compensate.
The learned policy is best understood as a \emph{local refinement within the basin defined by the teacher}, rather than an optimizer capable of global exploration.

\subsection{Why Calibrated UAS Outperforms UAS}

Calibrated UAS modifies three structural ingredients of UAS, each with a distinct effect:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Tempered normalization} ($\calbeta = 0.85$): the denominator $\srvrate_i^{\calbeta}$ grows more slowly than $\srvrate_i$, reducing the penalty differential between fast and slow servers.
\item \textbf{Softened prior} ($\calgamma = 0.5$): the pre-exponential factor $\srvrate_i^{\calgamma}$ flattens the routing bias toward fast servers relative to UAS's proportional-to-$\srvrate_i$ prior.
\item \textbf{Reduced look-ahead} ($\caloffset = 0.5$): the offset $\Qlen{i} + 0.5$ attenuates the anticipatory correction relative to UAS's $\Qlen{i} + 1$.
\end{itemize}
We conjecture that these modifications improve steady-state performance by rebalancing load distribution: the tempered normalization and softened prior jointly reduce over-routing to fast servers, while the reduced offset makes routing more responsive to current state.
The $1.45$-unit improvement over UAS ($10.04$ vs.\ $11.49$) confirms that meaningful empirical margin remains beyond the analytically tractable parameterization.

\subsection{Limitations}

\subsubsection{No Stability Proof for Calibrated UAS}
The weighted variational identity (Proposition~\ref{prop:uas_variational}) and the Jensen-based arrival bound (Lemma~\ref{lem:uas_arrival}) do not extend to the generalized parameterization.
Stability for general $(\calbeta, \calgamma, \caloffset)$ remains an open theoretical question.

\subsubsection{Default Pipeline Limitation}
The default $\ngibbsq$ model (UAS teacher) achieves $11.68$ in the anchor benchmark, below both JSSQ and Calibrated UAS.
The calibrated-teacher variant ($9.82$) is a separate model discovered through ablation.
Our results suggest that Calibrated UAS should be adopted as the default teacher in production deployments.

\subsubsection{Scale}
All primary comparisons use $\Nservers = 10$. The stress test validates stable behavior up to $\Nservers = 1{,}024$ (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_stress}), but competitive comparisons at larger $\Nservers$ are not reported and would strengthen the practical claims.

\subsubsection{Parameter Selection}
The Calibrated UAS parameters were selected via grid search on a separate validation set (Section~\ref{sec:calibrated_uas}), but the grid is coarse and the parameter surface may not be convex.
A more principled optimization procedure would strengthen the reported improvements.

\subsubsection{Gradient Magnitude}
The \reinforce{} estimator shows 32.6\% relative magnitude error versus finite differences (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_gradient}).
While directionally correct, the magnitude mismatch effectively reduces the learning rate at each step, potentially slowing convergence.
Improved variance reduction techniques (PPO, natural policy gradient) could address this limitation.

\subsection{Future Directions}

\subsubsection{Calibrated UAS Stability Analysis}
Extending the Lyapunov argument to cover the three-parameter family would close the gap between the analytical and empirical layers.

\subsubsection{Scale Experiments}
Evaluating competitive policy comparisons at $\Nservers \in \{50, 100\}$ would test whether the learned-policy advantage persists at scale.

\subsubsection{Advanced RL Algorithms}
PPO, SAC, or natural policy gradient methods could improve both training stability and gradient estimation fidelity.

\subsubsection{Non-Stationarity}
Time-varying arrival rates and dynamic server availability would test the framework's practical applicability.
