
\begin{abstract}
Routing policies for heterogeneous parallel queues face a fundamental
tension. Classical policies are either state-aware but oblivious to capacity,
or capacity-aware but blind to state, while reinforcement learning (RL)
approaches lack formal stability guarantees. We propose GibbsQ, a framework
that addresses this gap via entropy-regularized routing. We prove
positive Harris recurrence for raw softmax and UAS under the strict load
condition ($\totcap > \arrate$), and for a stability-certified calibrated
subclass (SCUAS) under an explicit parameter-conditional Foster--Lyapunov
drift bound. The raw softmax proof uses a quadratic Lyapunov function and an
entropy ceiling bound; the UAS proof uses a capacity-weighted Lyapunov
function and a prior-weighted KL variational identity; the SCUAS proof uses a
matched weighted Lyapunov function and a calibrated prior decomposition. These
proofs yield explicit, computable drift constants; the UAS constants are
temperature independent. Building on these foundations, we introduce
Calibrated UAS, a closed-form empirical extension of UAS with three tunable
parameters. Despite lacking a blanket formal stability proof over its full
parameter range, it reduces steady-state queue lengths by 8.9\% and 12.6\%
relative to JSSQ and baseline UAS, respectively, under the standard evaluation
protocol. A post-validation audit of the anchor benchmark finds that the
default calibrated setting and the three audited SCUAS grid candidates all
yield negative values of the sufficient drift constant
$\varepsilon_{\calbeta,\calgamma,\caloffset}$, so the calibrated benchmark
results remain empirical rather than theorem-certified in this study. We
also identify a benchmark-specific piecewise Lyapunov candidate whose exact
generator is strictly negative on all $9.85$ million states in the boundary
shells $|\Qstate|_1 \in \{16,17,18\}$ and on additional sampled higher-load
states, which strengthens the case for a future full calibrated proof without
itself constituting one. We
further observe a teacher-choice effect: an
RL agent initialized through behavior cloning from Calibrated UAS and
fine-tuned via \reinforce{} attains the lowest queue length among the policies
evaluated in this study, surpassing even its closed-form teacher, while an
identical architecture trained from random initialization diverges
catastrophically. This learned-policy advantage persists across the 16
operating configurations tested and into near-critical regimes up to
$\rho = 0.98$.
\end{abstract}
