
\section{Experimental Evaluation}
\label{sec:experiments}

This section presents the complete empirical evaluation of the GibbsQ framework.
All experiments use a NumPy-based event-driven CTMC simulation as the canonical evaluation engine.

\subsection{Experimental Protocol}
\label{sec:exp:setup}

\subsubsection{Common Evaluation Protocol}
Unless otherwise noted, all experiments use $\Nservers = 10$ heterogeneous servers with service rates drawn from a fixed configuration, load factor $\rho = \arrate/\totcap = 0.80$, and 32 independent replications per policy.
Configuration is managed via Hydra YAML profiles for full reproducibility.

\subsubsection{Primary Metric}
All comparisons use the steady-state mean total queue length $\E\bigl[\sum_{i=1}^{\Nservers} \Qlen{i}\bigr]$; lower is better.
We also report mean sojourn time and a queue-length Gini coefficient where they aid interpretation.

\subsubsection{Computational Cost}
The closed-form policies (UAS, Calibrated UAS, JSQ, JSSQ) compute routing probabilities in $O(\Nservers)$ time via vectorized operations, requiring $<\!10\,\mu\text{s}$ per routing decision at $\Nservers = 10$.
The neural policy requires a forward pass through a two-layer feedforward network, taking approximately $0.1$--$0.5\,\text{ms}$ per decision depending on batch size.
At $\Nservers = 1{,}024$, UAS routing remains below $100\,\mu\text{s}$; neural forward-pass cost scales linearly with input dimension.

Calibrated UAS ranks first among all closed-form methods, reducing mean queue length by 8.8\% relative to JSSQ ($10.04$ vs.\ $11.02$) and by 12.6\% relative to UAS.
The left panel of Figure~\ref{fig:policy} shows a pronounced separation between the high-performing policy group (Calibrated UAS, JSSQ, UAS, $\ngibbsq$, JSQ) and the poorly performing baselines (Proportional, Uniform).
The right panel confirms that sojourn-time rankings mirror queue-length rankings, as expected from Little's law.
Notably, JSQ achieves the lowest Gini coefficient ($0.074$) but the highest queue length among competitive policies, illustrating the cost of ignoring service-rate heterogeneity.

The default $\ngibbsq$ model, trained with UAS as the behavior-cloning teacher, achieves $11.68$---competitive with JSSQ and UAS but below Calibrated UAS.
This motivates the ablation study below, which identifies the teacher-policy bottleneck.

Three findings emerge:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Teacher choice dominates.}
  Switching the behavior-cloning teacher from UAS to Calibrated UAS reduces the final learned policy's mean queue from $11.14$ to $9.82$---a $1.32$-unit improvement that exceeds the effect of any architectural modification tested.
\item \textbf{Warm start is essential.}
  \reinforce{} from scratch yields a mean queue of $5{,}451$, confirming that behavior-cloning pretraining is a critical prerequisite for stable policy-gradient training in this domain.
\item \textbf{The calibrated-teacher variant is the overall best.}
  At $9.82$, it surpasses both the Calibrated UAS reference ($9.90$) and JSSQ ($10.83$) under this protocol, making it the strongest policy in the ablation suite.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Statistical Comparison}
\label{sec:exp:stats}

To quantify the performance gap with formal statistical rigor, we conduct a two-sample comparison between the default learned policy ($\ngibbsq$ with UAS teacher) and Calibrated UAS.
Table~\ref{tab:stats} reports the results; Figure~\ref{fig:stats} shows the distributions.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\small

\begin{tabular}{@{\hspace{6pt}}l @{\hspace{20pt}} r@{\hspace{6pt}}}

Relative improvement                        & 23.24\% \\

\begin{figure}[htbp!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth, height=0.35\textheight, keepaspectratio]{stats_boxplot.png}
\caption{\textbf{Statistical comparison} ($\Nservers{=}10$, 32 replications per policy).

\label{fig:stats}

The effect size $|d| = 26.3$ is exceptionally large by conventional standards (where $|d| > 0.8$ is considered ``large'').
This extreme value arises because each replication averages over thousands of arrival and service events, producing very low per-replication variance.
In this low-noise regime, even a moderate absolute mean difference (${\sim}3.5$ queue-length units) yields an enormous standardized effect size.
The violin plots in Figure~\ref{fig:stats} confirm this interpretation: both distributions are tightly concentrated with no overlap.

\subsection{Near-Critical Load Behavior}
\label{sec:exp:critical}

\subsection{Theory-Consistency Validations}
\label{sec:exp:theory}

Two validation experiments connect the empirical results to the analytical stability claims.

\subsubsection{Drift Verification}
The Foster--Lyapunov drift condition for UAS (Theorem~\ref{thm:uas}) was numerically verified by computing $\gen \lyap_{\mathrm{UAS}}(\Qstate)$ at 20{,}001 sampled states.
Zero bound violations were observed, with computed constants $\varepsilon = 0.28$ and $R = 38.39$ (petite-set radius $R/\varepsilon \approx 137$).
Full details are in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_drift}.

\subsubsection{Gradient Estimator Verification}
The \reinforce{} gradient estimator was validated against a symmetric finite-difference approximation, achieving cosine similarity of $1.0000$ (perfect directional agreement).
The 32.6\% relative magnitude error reflects the difference in sample budget between the two estimators and affects learning-rate calibration but not optimization direction.
Full details are in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_gradient}.
